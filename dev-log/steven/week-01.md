# AI-Assisted Development Log

Name: Steven Fang
Week: Week 1 (May 27 – June 3, 2026)
Date: 2026-06-03

## 1. Task / Goal
[ux/product] Define the first version of the Empress of Japan visitor experience. The goal was to turn the broad museum prompt into a concrete interaction concept: visitors enter a ship-based immersive experience, choose a narrator, move through historical spaces, and learn through voice-based storytelling rather than a static information page. This week focused on experience vision, user journey mapping, narrator concept, story structure, and initial mockups that the team could use to align around the experience.

## 2. AI Tools Used
ChatGPT, Claude, and image generation tools.

## 3. Prompts / Agent Workflow
I used AI mostly as a design critique and iteration partner rather than as an automatic generator. The workflow was:
1. **Experience framing** — used AI to compare possible experience models: traditional exhibit website, CYOA narrative, voice-based character interaction, and WebXR-style exploration.
2. **Narrative exploration** — tested different narrator roles and story structures to see which ones gave the clearest contrast in class, labor, and authority aboard the ship.
3. **Visual reference generation** — generated early environment and character references to help communicate the intended mood before the final art direction was fixed.
4. **Writing and alignment support** — used AI to refine sprint language, project descriptions, and stakeholder-facing wording so the concept could be explained clearly.

## 4. Useful Output
- Initial end-to-end visitor flow: physical exhibit → QR/kiosk entry → narrator selection → ship environment → voice interaction → artifact/story discovery.
- Early character direction for three narrator perspectives: first-class passenger, captain/senior officer, and lower-deck crew member.
- Drafted descriptions for the "Choose Your Voyage" concept and the voice-based AI interaction.
- Early visual references for ship interiors, avatar style, and historical atmosphere.
- Standup/retro wording that connected UX work to the team's broader product direction.

## 5. Human Review / Changes
- **Rejected a pure information-system direction.** The first risk was making the project feel like a normal museum website with facts and images. I pushed the concept toward story, perspective, and interaction instead.
- **Narrowed the narrator set myself.** AI could suggest many possible characters, but I selected the final structure because it created useful contrast: privilege, authority, and labor.
- **Corrected tone and historical sensitivity.** Some early character naming and wording felt too stereotyped or too modern, especially for the Asian crew member. I changed the character to Ming Chen and kept the tone practical, human, and less racialized.
- **Kept the scope grounded.** Some ideas sounded interesting but would have turned the project into a full game. I kept the flow focused on museum use: short interaction, clear entry point, and meaningful historical takeaway.
- **Rewrote AI-sounding language.** Several AI drafts were too generic, so I simplified the wording to sound closer to how I would actually explain the project.

## 6. Reflection
The most useful part of AI this week was speed: it helped me explore many directions quickly and see which ones sounded weak, generic, or unrealistic. The less reliable part was judgment. AI often produced polished-sounding language that did not reflect the actual project constraints, so I had to cut a lot of it. The main lesson was that defining the product came before polishing the design. Once the experience was framed around narrator perspectives and interactive storytelling, the UI and visual direction became much easier to organize.
